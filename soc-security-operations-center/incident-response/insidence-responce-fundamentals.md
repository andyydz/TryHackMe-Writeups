> **Key Finding:** A single employee executing a malicious email attachment can compromise an entire organisation — structured incident response frameworks exist to detect, contain, and recover from exactly this scenario.

## Overview
This room covers the fundamentals of incident response — what incidents are, how alerts are triggered, and how security teams respond using established frameworks. Includes a hands-on lab where I investigated a malicious email attachment incident, identifying the sender, affected devices, and the employee who executed the payload.

## Hands-On Activities

### Understanding Incidents and Alerts
Studied the difference between true positives and false positives — a critical skill for SOC analysts who must triage alerts efficiently without wasting time on benign events.

### Incident Types
Reviewed the five major incident categories:
- **Malware Infection** — malicious software executing on endpoints
- **Security Breach** — unauthorised access to systems or data
- **Data Leak** — sensitive data exposed internally or externally
- **Insider Attack** — threat originating from within the organisation
- **Denial of Service** — overwhelming a system to disrupt availability

### SANS vs NIST IR Frameworks

| Phase | SANS | NIST |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Preparation | Preparation |
| 2 | Identification | Detection & Analysis |
| 3 | Containment | Containment, Eradication & Recovery |
| 4 | Eradication | Post-Incident Activity |
| 5 | Recovery | — |
| 6 | Lessons Learned | — |

### Incident Response Techniques & Tools
Explored the key tools used by security teams during IR:
- **SIEM** — aggregates and correlates logs from across the environment to surface threats
- **EDR (Endpoint Detection & Response)** — monitors endpoints for suspicious behaviour
- **Antivirus** — signature-based detection of known malware
- **SOAR/Automation** — accelerates response through playbook-driven actions

### Lab — Malicious Email Attachment Investigation
Worked through a simulated incident involving a phishing email with a malicious attachment:
- Identified the **malicious email sender**
- Confirmed the attachment was a **genuine threat** (true positive)
- Found that **3 devices downloaded** the attachment
- Determined that **1 employee executed** the payload — leading to a confirmed compromise
- Traced the affected employee and retrieved the **flag**

## Key Takeaways

- Logs are the foundation of incident response — without them, detection and investigation are impossible
- True positives require immediate action; false positives must be tuned out to reduce alert fatigue
- SANS and NIST provide structured IR frameworks — both start with preparation and end with post-incident review
- SIEM is the central tool in a SOC — it collects, correlates, and surfaces threats from across the environment
- A single employee executing a malicious attachment can be the entry point for a full organisation compromise
- Incident scoping matters — knowing how many devices downloaded vs executed a payload defines containment priority
- Phishing via email attachment remains one of the most common and effective initial access techniques
